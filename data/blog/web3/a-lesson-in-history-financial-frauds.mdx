## The Great Depression

![Great Depression](/static/images/blogs/great_depression_1929.jpg)

The 1929 financial crisis, also known as the [Great Depression](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Great_Depression), was a severe economic downturn that occurred in the United States and had ripple effects worldwide.

### What happened <Twemoji emoji="thinking-face" />?

During the 1920s, the United States experienced a period of economic growth, and many people were investing in the stock market. Stock prices were rising rapidly, and people believed they could make easy money by buying and selling stocks.

The booming economy gave rise to a speculative market. Speculators were individuals or groups who bought stocks with the expectation of selling them quickly at higher prices to make a profit. Their actions drove up stock prices to unsustainable levels, creating an artificial bubble in the market.

Many ordinary people invested their savings in the stock market, hoping to benefit from the booming economy. They purchased stocks based on the belief that prices would continue to rise, fueled by the optimism of the time. When the market crashed, these investors suffered significant losses as stock values plummeted.

Banks were heavily involved in the stock market during the 1920s. They lent money to investors and speculators, allowing them to buy stocks on margin. Margin trading involves borrowing money to invest, using the stocks themselves as collateral. When stock prices fell, investors couldn't repay their loans, leading to a wave of defaults. Many banks suffered significant losses and eventually failed.

Stockbrokers facilitated the buying and selling of stocks for their clients. During the speculative frenzy, some stockbrokers engaged in unethical practices such as promoting risky investments and manipulating stock prices. They often provided loans to speculators, further inflating the bubble. When the crash occurred, many brokers and brokerage firms faced financial ruin.

The government and regulatory bodies of the time were criticized for their lack of oversight and regulation in the financial markets. There were no strict rules in place to prevent excessive speculation or protect investors from fraudulent practices. The absence of effective regulation allowed the bubble to grow unchecked, eventually leading to its collapse.

It's important to note that the 1929 financial crisis was a complex event influenced by multiple factors and actors. While these entities played a role, the crash itself was the result of a culmination of unsustainable practices, overvaluation of stocks, and a lack of proper financial safeguards.

The aftermath of the crisis prompted significant regulatory reforms, including the establishment of the Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC) in the United States. These reforms aimed to enhance transparency, regulate stock market activities, and prevent future financial crises.

### Lessons <Twemoji emoji="light-bulb" />

The Great Depression of 1929 taught us the critical need for transparency, accountability, and prudent financial practices. Excessive speculation, unethical behavior, and lack of oversight led to catastrophic economic collapse.

## The Great Recession

> Recommendation: there are two exceptional movies on the event - [The Big Short](<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Big_Short_(film)>) and [Margin Call](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Margin_Call).

![Great Recession](/static/images/blogs/great_recession_2008.jpg)

The 2008 financial crisis, also known as the global financial crisis or the [Great Recession](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Great_Recession), was a severe economic downturn that began in the United States and spread across the world. It was triggered by a combination of factors that ultimately led to a collapse in the housing market and a subsequent crisis in the financial sector.

### What happened <Twemoji emoji="thinking-face" />?

In the early 2000s, the demand for housing in the U.S. surged, leading banks to lend money to people with poor credit histories. These risky loans were packaged into [mortgage-backed securities (MBS)](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/m/mbs.asp) and sold to investors. To complicate matters, these MBS were bundled into complex financial products known as [collateralized debt obligations (CDOs)](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/c/cdo.asp).

As time passed, the housing bubble burst. Housing prices declined, and borrowers defaulted on their mortgage payments. This caused a sharp drop in the value of MBS and CDOs held by financial institutions. Many banks and financial firms had heavily invested in these securities, assuming housing prices would keep rising. When the market crashed, their investments plummeted in value, leading to massive losses.

The crisis escalated when it became clear that many financial institutions had engaged in risky and irresponsible practices. Some banks gave out loans without properly assessing borrowers' ability to repay, while others engaged in predatory lending. Additionally, financial institutions took on excessive leverage, borrowing large sums to finance their investments, making them highly vulnerable to losses.

As the crisis unfolded, several major financial institutions faced insolvency or collapse. Lehman Brothers, one of the largest investment banks, filed for bankruptcy, causing widespread panic in the financial markets. Governments and central banks stepped in to prevent further collapses by providing massive bailouts and injecting liquidity into the system (**_make a mental note of this as this is going to be a key point which we will discuss in later posts_**).

The consequences were severe. Many people lost their homes, jobs, and savings. Stock markets plummeted, leading to a global economic downturn. Governments implemented various measures to stabilize the financial system and stimulate economic growth.

### Lessons <Twemoji emoji="light-bulb" />

The 2008 financial crisis exposed flaws in the regulatory and oversight systems of the financial industry. It highlighted the interconnectedness of financial institutions and the risks associated with excessive speculation and lack of transparency. In response, governments and regulatory bodies implemented reforms to strengthen oversight and improve risk management practices in the financial sector.

Overall, the 2008 financial crisis serves as a stark reminder of the dangers of unchecked risk-taking, inadequate regulation, and the potential for a collapse in the financial system to have far-reaching effects on the global economy.

## Hyperinflation in Zimbabwe

![Hyperinflation in Zimbabwe](/static/images/blogs/hyperinflation_in_zimbabwe.jpg)

Hyperinflation in Zimbabwe refers to an extreme period of rapidly escalating prices that occurred in the late 2000s and early 2010s, severely impacting the economy and its people.

During hyperinflation, the value of money diminishes rapidly, causing prices for goods and services to skyrocket. Essentially, the money individuals have becomes increasingly less valuable, making it challenging to afford basic necessities.

### What happened <Twemoji emoji="thinking-face" />?

Zimbabwe's hyperinflation stemmed from a combination of factors, including government economic mismanagement, excessive money printing, and overall economic instability. The government spent beyond its means and resorted to printing more money to cover deficits, flooding the economy with excess currency. Consequently, this oversupply devalued the Zimbabwean dollar.

This economic turmoil led to staggering increases in prices for essential items such as food, clothing, and housing. Imagine waking up to find the cost of bread has doubled or tripled overnight. Such rapid price hikes characterized the everyday reality during hyperinflation.

The consequences were devastating: savings lost their value, purchasing power plummeted, and both individuals and businesses struggled to make transactions and plan for the future. Many fell into poverty, and economic instability became pervasive.

To combat this crisis, Zimbabwe abandoned its currency in favor of foreign alternatives like the U.S. dollar and South African rand for everyday transactions, which stabilized prices somewhat. However, the long-term repercussions of hyperinflation continued to haunt the economy.

Throughout this crisis, ordinary Zimbabweans bore the brunt of these policy failures. Their savings evaporated, purchasing power eroded, and many were pushed into poverty. Despite these profound impacts on citizens' lives, the decision-makers responsible often escaped accountability.

This episode underscores a broader issue: governments can make decisions that inflict significant harm on their populations without facing meaningful consequences. It highlights the critical need for transparency, accountability, and responsible governance in safeguarding economic stability and protecting the well-being of citizens.

### Lessons <Twemoji emoji="light-bulb" />

Hyperinflation in Zimbabwe stands as a stark example of how unchecked money supply and economic mismanagement can devastate a nation and its people. The government's decisions, including excessive money printing and unsustainable spending, led to rapid devaluation of the currency and soaring prices for basic goods.

## GameStop Short Squeeze and Robinhood

> Recommendation: an excellent movie on the incident - [Dumb Money](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dumb_Money)

![GameStop Short Squeeze and Robinhood](/static/images/blogs/gamestop_short_squeeze.jpg)

The GameStop short squeeze of early 2021 became a notable event highlighting the power of retail investors and the controversies surrounding trading platforms.

### What happened <Twemoji emoji="thinking-face" />?

In January 2021, a group of individual investors on a social media platform called Reddit started buying shares of a struggling video game retailer called **GameStop**. They believed that large Wall Street investors were unfairly short-selling the stock, which means they were betting that the price of the stock would go down.

As more and more individual investors started buying shares of GameStop, the stock price began to rise rapidly. This created a situation where the Wall Street investors who had bet against the stock, known as hedge funds, were losing a lot of money.

In response to this, some online brokerages, including Robinhood, decided to restrict or limit the buying of certain stocks, including GameStop. They argued that they did this to protect their users and manage the risk associated with the highly volatile market activity.

This decision sparked a lot of controversy and anger among the individual investors who felt that they were being treated unfairly. Many accused Robinhood of siding with the Wall Street hedge funds and limiting access to the market for ordinary people.

The scandal brought attention to questions of market manipulation, the influence of large financial institutions, and the role of online trading platforms. It also raised concerns about whether these platforms have the best interests of their users in mind.

Robinhood faced backlash from its users and even received criticism from lawmakers and regulators. They argued that access to the markets should be fair and equal for all investors, and that decisions like restricting trading should be transparent and justified.

### Lessons <Twemoji emoji="light-bulb" />

The GameStop saga underscored the complexities of modern financial markets and the power dynamics between retail and institutional investors. It raised questions about market manipulation, regulatory oversight, and the responsibilities of trading platforms.

Moreover, it highlighted the need for transparency and equitable access to financial markets. The incident prompted calls for reforms to ensure that all investors, regardless of their size or influence, can participate fairly and without undue interference.

## Satyam Scandal

![Satyam Scandal](/static/images/blogs/satyam_scandal.jpg)

Satyam Computer Services Ltd. was an Indian IT services and consulting company based in Hyderabad. Founded by Ramalinga Raju in 1987, Satyam grew rapidly and became one of the top IT companies in India, with a presence in over 60 countries.

### What happened <Twemoji emoji="thinking-face" />?

On January 7, 2009, Ramalinga Raju, the chairman of Satyam, confessed to massive financial fraud in a letter to the company's board of directors. He admitted to falsifying accounts and inflating profits for several years.

Raju revealed that Satyam's balance sheet included non-existent cash and bank balances of over ₹5,000 crore (approximately USD 1 billion) and understated liabilities. The gap between the actual and reported figures was significant, leading to a financial hole that was impossible to fill.

Raju and his associates created fictitious revenue entries, forging invoices and client contracts to show higher sales. They fabricated bank statements and fixed deposit receipts to show non-existent cash reserves. The company's liabilities were grossly understated, masking its true financial position.

Raju initially claimed that the fraud started as a small cover-up to avoid negative market perception but spiraled out of control over the years. He also suggested that he intended to rectify the situation but could not find a way out.

The confession led to a massive sell-off of Satyam's stock, causing its share price to plummet by nearly 80 percent in a single day.

The Indian stock market also suffered a significant decline, eroding investor confidence.

Satyam's board was immediately disbanded, and the Indian government stepped in to appoint new directors to stabilize the company.

### Lessons <Twemoji emoji="light-bulb" />

The Satyam scandal serves as a stark reminder of the potential for corporate fraud and the devastating impact it can have on stakeholders. It also highlights the ongoing need for vigilance, transparency, and accountability in the corporate world to maintain trust and integrity in financial markets.

## Need for Robust Financial Solution

The historical financial crises and scandals we have examined reveal profound vulnerabilities within our economic systems. These events underscore the necessity for a solution that ensures several critical attributes:

### Transparency

Clear and accessible financial information is paramount to prevent manipulation and build trust. In the absence of transparency, as seen in the **Great Depression** and the **Satyam Scandal**, stakeholders are left in the dark, unable to make informed decisions. Transparent systems ensure that all transactions and financial data are visible and verifiable, making it difficult for deceptive practices to go unnoticed.

### Accountability

Mechanisms that hold entities responsible for their actions are essential for deterring fraud. During the **Great Recession**, the lack of accountability allowed financial institutions to engage in reckless lending and trading practices without immediate repercussions. Effective accountability ensures that individuals and organizations are answerable for their actions, providing a strong deterrent against unethical behavior.

### Security

Robust systems that protect against fraud and ensure the integrity of financial transactions are critical. The **hyperinflation in Zimbabwe** and the \*\*GameStop Short Squeeze\*\* both illustrate the chaos that can ensue when financial systems are insecure and easily manipulated. Secure financial infrastructures prevent unauthorized access and alterations, maintaining the stability and reliability of financial operations.

### Decentralization

Reducing the concentration of power is crucial to prevent systemic failures and manipulation. Centralized systems, as demonstrated in multiple financial crises, are vulnerable to the failings of a few influential entities. Decentralized systems distribute power and control, reducing the risk of catastrophic failures and ensuring that no single entity can dominate or manipulate the entire system.
